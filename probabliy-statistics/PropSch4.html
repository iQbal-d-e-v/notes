📊 How to Present Large Data in Compact Form

Overview

Imagine you have a giant bag of numbers—thousands of marks scored by students in an exam. If I give you the full list, you’ll get lost. So, in statistics, we don’t just dump data—we organize it so that patterns become visible at a glance.

There are three powerful ways to do this:

  • Histogram – like a bar graph for continuous data
  • Ogive (Cumulative Frequency Curve) – like watching numbers “add up” step by step
  • Stem-and-Leaf Plot – like a mini-database that keeps original numbers visible

1️⃣ Histogram

Think of a histogram like a building skyline in a city. Each building represents how many numbers fall into a certain range (class interval).

Example: Suppose exam marks range from 0 to 100. We divide them into intervals:

  • 0–19, 20–39, 40–59, 60–79, 80–100

Now, count how many students fall into each group:

  • 0–19 → 2 students
  • 20–39 → 5 students
  • 40–59 → 10 students
  • 60–79 → 8 students
  • 80–100 → 5 students

On a graph:

  • x-axis = class intervals (marks ranges)
  • y-axis = frequency (number of students)
  • Draw bars with no gaps

✅ The histogram immediately tells you where most students scored. (Looks like most are in the 40–59 range).

2️⃣ Ogive (Cumulative Frequency Curve)

The word ogive may sound fancy, but it’s simple. It’s like saying: “How many students scored up to this mark?”

Using the same data:

  • Up to 19 → 2 students
  • Up to 39 → 2 + 5 = 7 students
  • Up to 59 → 7 + 10 = 17 students
  • Up to 79 → 17 + 8 = 25 students
  • Up to 100 → 25 + 5 = 30 students

Now plot:

  • x-axis = upper class boundaries (19, 39, 59, 79, 100)
  • y-axis = cumulative frequency (2, 7, 17, 25, 30)
  • Connect the points smoothly to form a rising curve

✅ This curve helps us find medians, quartiles, percentiles—things newspapers use when they say: “Top 25% of students scored above 80 marks.”

3️⃣ Stem-and-Leaf Plot

Now imagine instead of graphing, you want to see the actual numbers but in an organized way. That’s where the stem-and-leaf plot comes in. Think of it like a tree: the “stem” is the main trunk (tens digit), and the “leaves” are hanging (ones digit).

Example marks: 19, 22, 25, 42, 45, 50, 65, 77, 90

We split them:

  • Stem = tens digit
  • Leaf = ones digit
Stem | Leaves
  1  | 9
  2  | 2, 5
  4  | 2, 5
  5  | 0
  6  | 5
  7  | 7
  9  | 0

✅ With one glance, you can see:

  • Lowest = 19
  • Highest = 90
  • Cluster around 40–50

It’s like a histogram without losing the raw data.

🌟 Why These Are Powerful

  • Histogram → shows the shape of data (are scores high, low, spread out?)
  • Ogive → shows how data accumulates (good for medians/percentiles)
  • Stem-and-Leaf → compact but detailed snapshot (raw data + grouping)

That’s why newspapers, TV channels, and researchers all use these. They take huge piles of data and convert them into something our brains can digest quickly.
